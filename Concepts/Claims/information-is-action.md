Donald MacKay reconceptualized information as an action rather than a thing. Information is verb-like: a process that someone enacts. This stands in contrast to [[Claude-Shannon]]'s statistical definition, which quantifies information independent of meaning and context but established [[negentropy]] as a measurable quantity.

MacKay's formulation necessarily implies context and embodiment. Information arises when an agent performs an act of distinction within a specific situation. The price for this embodiment is difficulty of quantification and loss of universality. [[Claude-Shannon]] gained mathematical precision by excluding meaning; MacKay regained meaning at the cost of formal tractability.

This positions MacKay between [[Gregory-Bateson]] and [[tube-metaphor-is-false]]. Bateson likewise insisted that information depends on an observer's act of distinction. The tube metaphor critique argues that meaning is enacted at reception, not carried in the signal. MacKay adds that the act of informing is itself a kind of doing: information does not represent action; it is action.

[[agency-is-verb-not-noun]] parallels this verb-like conception at the level of causality and being. Agency is enacted through relation, as information is enacted through distinction. Neither is a property you possess; both are processes you participate in.
