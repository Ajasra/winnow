Gordon Pask developed Conversation Theory, proposing how humans, devices, and their shared environments might coexist in a mutually constructive relationship. His central concern: if a designer specifies all parts and behaviors at the beginning, the eventual identity is limited by what the designer can predict.

MusiColour, his interactive light-and-sound system, operationalized this principle. The system becomes bored when performers repeat patterns and starts listening for other frequency ranges. This boredom response forces novelty from the human participant.

The human component anchors the model. A person engaged in determining input/output criteria productively converses with their environment. Pask's design goal: tools that people use to construct their environments and build their own sense of agency. This positions the designer as an enabler of emergence rather than a specifier of outcomes. The approach informs [[systems-aesthetics]].

[[cognitive-homeostasis]] and [[symbiomemesis]] directly inherit Pask's boredom mechanism: the agent maintains artificial vitality by seeking perturbation when input becomes predictable. [[Deleuze-Guattari]]'s [[desiring-machines]] parallel this: the capacity for unlimited connections in all directions, dissolving the boundary between organism and machine. [[software-art]] inherits Pask's principle that the state of the system at one moment is a function of its state in the moment just passed.
