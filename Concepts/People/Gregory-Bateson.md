Gregory Bateson defined information as "a difference that makes a difference." This formulation binds information to an observer capable of registering the difference. Without a system that distinguishes, no difference becomes information.

His cybernetic epistemology linked communication, mind, and nature into a unified systems framework, developed in *Steps to an Ecology of Mind*. Art holds a special role in this framework: it corrects a too-purposive view of life by making perception more systemic. Works of art carry large unconscious components. Skill indicates the presence of large unconscious components in performance. Isadora Duncan captured the ineffability principle: "If I could tell you what it meant, there would be no point in dancing."

Bateson's definition of information contrasts with [[Claude-Shannon]]'s statistical framework, which intentionally excluded meaning. For Bateson, meaning cannot be separated from the act of distinction that generates the difference. This positions his work between [[tube-metaphor-is-false]] (communication depends on the receiver) and [[information-is-action]] (information as enacted process).

[[Karen-Barad]]'s [[agential-realism]] carries Bateson's cybernetic epistemology into quantum physics, treating mind, matter, and meaning as a single phenomenon rather than separate domains. His daughter [[Mary-Catherine-Bateson]] extended this epistemology into anthropology, crystallizing the recursive nature of selfhood: "Each person is his own central metaphor."
