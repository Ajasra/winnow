Communication is not generated at one point, carried by a conduit, and delivered to a receiver. The tube metaphor, central to [[Claude-Shannon]] and Weaver's transmission model, misrepresents how communication actually works. The phenomenon of communication depends on what happens to the person who receives it.

[[Maturana-Varela]] ground this claim in [[autopoiesis]]: each system is structurally determined, so the same signal triggers different [[structural-coupling|structural changes]] in different receivers. From an observer's perspective, there is always ambiguity in communicative interaction. What counts as information is determined at the point of reception.

Signals travel through channels; meaning does not travel with them. The sender's intent and the receiver's response are structurally coupled through the history of their interactions, rather than causally linked through the content of any single message. [[Gregory-Bateson]] reinforces this with his definition of information as a difference that makes a difference to a specific observing system.

[[intra-action]] reinforces this at the ontological level: communication is mutual constitution, not transmission between pre-formed entities. The tube metaphor fails because it assumes relata that preexist the communicative act.
