Norbert Wiener founded cybernetics in his 1948 book of the same name, establishing a science of control and communication applying to goal-seeking behavior of animals and machines. His core insight: feedback introduces circularity into systems. Once output feeds back as input, the observer cannot stand outside the observed.

Wiener identified hunting as uncontrolled oscillation in a system: when feedback breaks, the system fills with noise and begins oscillating wildly. Living organisms are pockets of order in a noisy universe. Media, in his analysis, carries noise meant to manipulate rather than inform.

This circularity is the foundation of [[second-order-cybernetics]]: the inclusion of the observer as an inextricable part of the system. Wiener's feedback loop prefigures [[Maturana-Varela]]'s [[autopoiesis]], where structural coupling between system and environment operates through mutual perturbation rather than causal instruction. His work also informs [[Gregory-Bateson]]'s cybernetic epistemology, which treats mind and nature as a single interconnected circuit. [[Donella-Meadows]] extended Wiener's insight into systems dynamics: giving systems better information improves them because they self-organize toward coherence.
