Second-order cybernetics includes the observer as an inextricable part of the system, introducing radical reflexivity. First-order cybernetics studied control and communication in goal-seeking behavior of animals and machines. The second-order turn rejects the premise that an observer can stand outside what is observed.

[[Norbert-Wiener]] identified the root problem: feedback introduces circularity. Once a system feeds output back as input, the boundary between observer and observed dissolves. [[Mary-Catherine-Bateson]] crystallized this: "Each person is his own central metaphor." All errors about the observed become errors about the observer.

The epistemic consequence is [[constructivism]]: what we observe is not nature itself but nature exposed to our method of questioning. This aligns with [[autopoiesis]]: the observer's structure determines what perturbations register as information. [[Gregory-Bateson]] extended this logic into cybernetic epistemology, treating mind and nature as a single interconnected system.

[[Karen-Barad]]'s [[agential-realism]] converges with this position from quantum physics: Niels Bohr showed that the apparatus participates in constituting the phenomenon, paralleling the cybernetic observer's inextricable role in the system.
