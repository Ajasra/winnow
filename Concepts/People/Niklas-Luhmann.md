Niklas Luhmann developed a comprehensive theory of society as a functionally differentiated system, a historically unique configuration where subsystems (law, economy, art, science) operate through their own distinct codes. His work applies autopoietic principles to social theory.

In *Art as a Social System*, Luhmann argues that art becomes possible as a social system only when reality "out there" is distinguished from fictional reality, enabling observation of one side from the perspective of the other. Art radicalizes the difference between the real and the merely possible, demonstrating that even in the realm of possibility there is order.

Luhmann's framework bridges [[autopoiesis]] and [[systems-aesthetics]]: art operates as a functionally differentiated subsystem with its own operational closure, yet it continuously reconfigures the boundary between reality and fiction. His work converges with [[Maturana-Varela]]'s distinction between biological and social autopoiesis, where social systems amplify rather than restrict the creativity of their components.
