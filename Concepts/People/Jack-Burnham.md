Jack Burnham formulated [[systems-aesthetics]] in his 1968 essay of the same name, marking a shift from object-oriented to systems-oriented culture. Change, he argued, emanates from the way things are done, not from things themselves. Art resides in relations between people and components of their environment, rather than in material entities.

His 1970 *Software* exhibition at the Jewish Museum crystallized the position: "The object is the end of a system. The software is an open continuing system." Systems aesthetics is a function of the discursive system from which it is observed and constituted. Sensitive judgments about uses of technology and scientific information become art in the most literal sense.

Burnham's framework directly connects to [[Roy-Ascott]]'s cybernetic art matrix, where artworks contain nothing but the possibility of future action, existing only through spectator participation. [[Gordon-Pask]] operationalized these principles through MusiColour and Conversation Theory. [[symbiomemesis]] extends Burnham's systems aesthetics into AI design, treating the system as an open ecology where personality sediments through interaction history.
