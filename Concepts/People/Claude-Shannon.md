Claude Shannon, with Warren Weaver, authored *The Mathematical Theory of Communication* (1949), establishing the statistical framework for information theory. The fundamental problem: reproducing at one point a message selected at another point.

Shannon's model operates at three levels: technical (accuracy of transmission), semantic (meaning of transmitted symbols), and effectiveness (impact of received meaning on conduct). His definition quantifies information as a choice from a range of possible messages, deliberately excluding meaning to achieve mathematical precision. A concept that ties information to meaning would yield different values for different contexts.

This exclusion of meaning positions Shannon as the primary counterpoint to [[Gregory-Bateson]], who defined information as "a difference that makes a difference" inseparable from the observer who registers it. Shannon's framework also underlies [[negentropy]]: information increases structure and potential energy within a system, and exchange between systems of different information content amplifies rather than equalizes. [[Donald-MacKay]] proposed information as an enacted process requiring context and embodiment. The [[tube-metaphor-is-false|tube metaphor]] critique targets Shannon's transmission model directly: communication depends on reception, not on channel capacity. [[Maturana-Varela]] inverted Shannon's framework by centering the receiver's structural determination over the sender's encoding.
