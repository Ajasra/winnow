Donald MacKay reconceptualized information as an action rather than a thing. Information is verb-like: a process that someone enacts within a specific context. This formulation necessarily implies embodiment, standing in contrast to [[Claude-Shannon]]'s statistical definition, which deliberately excluded meaning to achieve mathematical precision.

MacKay argued that information arises when an agent performs an act of distinction within a situation. The price for this embodiment is difficulty of quantification and loss of universality. Shannon gained precision by excluding meaning; MacKay regained meaning at the cost of formal tractability.

His work occupies the middle ground between [[Gregory-Bateson]], who defined information as a difference that makes a difference to an observer, and the [[tube-metaphor-is-false|tube metaphor critique]], which argues that meaning is enacted at reception rather than carried in the signal. [[information-is-action|Information as action]] extends MacKay's core insight: the act of informing is itself a kind of doing. [[agency-is-verb-not-noun]] parallels this verb-like conception at the level of causality and being.
