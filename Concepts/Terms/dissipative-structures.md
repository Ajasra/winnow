Dissipative structures are open systems that maintain organization through continuous exchange of energy and matter with their environment, operating far from thermodynamic equilibrium. Coined by Ilya Prigogine (Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 1977), the concept describes how order arises in conditions that classical thermodynamics predicts should produce disorder.

Living systems are dissipative structures: order is established by the system itself, not imposed from outside. The system imports energy, exports entropy, and sustains its internal organization against the gradient of thermodynamic decay. This is the physical mechanism underlying [[autopoiesis]] — the self-producing character of living systems.

Dissipative structures connect to [[negentropy]] and to [[second-order-cybernetics]]. [[Donella-Meadows]]'s dictum, "systems create their own behavior," describes the self-organizing character of dissipative systems.
