Roy Ascott theorized the cybernetic art matrix: a framework where anarchic group behavior interacts with pre-established communication systems, hardware, and learning nets. Artworks in this framework contain nothing but the possibility of future action, existing only insofar as the spectator participates.

Behavioral synthesis blurs the boundaries between music, poetry, painting, architecture, sculpture, and acting. The distinction between artist and audience dissolves. The artwork becomes a matrix between two sets of behavior that merge into one continuous, interrelated system. The observer of the artwork is a participator who, in accessing the system, transforms it.

Ascott's matrix extends [[Jack-Burnham]]'s [[systems-aesthetics]] by centering participation as the constitutive act. [[Gordon-Pask]] operationalized parallel principles through MusiColour, where the system's boredom response forced human novelty. [[symbiomemesis]] inherits Ascott's logic: the agent and participant form an open system where interaction continuously reshapes both, and the system's identity emerges through sustained participatory engagement.
